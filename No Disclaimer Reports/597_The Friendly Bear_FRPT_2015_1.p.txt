We don't know about you, but we absolutely love award shows. And with the Oscars and
Grammy's now both behind us, we have been starved for a new award show. So we
decided to create our own. We don't know much about movies or music, but we do know a
little bit about stock research.


So today, we are announcing the inaugural edition of the "All-Hype" research awards. The
All-Hype award is a special designation for sell-side analysts who have gone above and
beyond in their research to find new, creative, and innovative ways of propping the stock
prices of absurd companies.


Winners must do far more than just publish hyperbolic research. They must demonstrate
an ability to give life to stocks that, in the absence of sell-side coverage, would not even
be viable standalone public companies.


And today, we are excited to announce our first nominee to 2015's All-Hype Team - the
Goldman Sachs Consumer Packaged Goods Investment Research Team.


However, we are firm believers in the phrase "it takes a whole village to raise a child". In
the case of Freshpet (NASDAQ:FRPT), it appears to have taken a whole underwriting
group to prop up the stock. So we would also like to extend special recognition to the
broader sell-side community covering Freshpet that has rallied in support of this flimsy
IPO, misconstruing even the most marginally positive data point as the basis for stock
price upgrades.

Humor aside, we thought that the purpose of sell-side research is to analyze and assess
the fundamental strengths and weaknesses of a company. We didn't think the purpose of
sell-side research was to attack short sellers!


Perhaps we could understand the attacks if Freshpet's stock had been languishing
following its IPO. But with the stock sitting at a cool ~45% premium to its IPO price, we
cannot help but wonder why the sell side has taken such an aggressive stance against the
short interest in Freshpet's stock.


Take the time to browse through research reports on Freshpet, and you will see comment
after comment that either "refutes the bear thesis" or that outright claims the "shorts are
wrong".


In the case of Freshpet, we understand that the underwriters of this IPO are likely quite
sensitive to negative reports, given the sheer volume of short reports that were circulated
following the company's IPO, and given the flimsy nature of this company's business
model. In fact, we cannot even recall an instance in which the sell side has paid this level
of attention to short reports. The underwriter attack on the shorts itself should raise
serious red flags for anyone analyzing Freshpet.


In this article, we explain why we believe the sell side is so near-term focused on
squeezing the shorts. This article is also meant to be educational, as we think readers
would benefit from learning more about how misaligned incentives drive stock research
decisions. We also explore the reasons why we believe Freshpet's stock is going to crater
once the sell-side support for the stock languishes.

Not only are the comps irrelevant, but Goldman is valuing the business based on 2018
estimated EBITDA in order to justify its target price for Freshpet.


We cannot fathom how anyone other than a muppet would ever buy into this egregious
valuation framework.


Let's put Freshpet's valuation into context. HAIN, a human food CPG company that also
focuses on the organic food trend, has over $2.5B in revenues and trades for a ~$7B
valuation. The company is also highly profitable, generating over $300M of EBITDA
annually. Freshpet, on the other hand, has $87M in revenue and adjusted EBITDA of
$5.7M (we won't dive into the absurdity of Freshpet's adjusted EBITDA metric in this
article, but we remind readers that we have observed dozens of JOBS ACT IPOs that are
riddled with "adjustments" that would make even Enron blush).


In other words, Goldman wants you to believe that Freshpet is a conviction-list buy,
when it is a cash-burning machine that generates only 3% of HAIN's revenues, under 2%
of HAIN's EBITDA, yet has a valuation that currently represents over 10% of HAIN's
valuation. By this simple heuristic alone, Freshpet appears to be at least around 3-5x
overvalued.


The bulls on this name will now complain that our simple analysis ignores the "growth
potential at Freshpet".


So let's put Freshpet's supposed growth potential into context.


What if we told you that there was a retailer with over 13,000 locations that still
somehow lost money.


Any way you cut it - that is what Freshpet represents. It is a "store within a store" concept.
Each fridge represents a Freshpet "store location". In other words, Freshpet may have set
a record for generating the most atrocious level of profitability ever seen in a company
with such a broad store footprint. The company's future growth needs to be looked at in
context of its already massive store footprint. In fact, if you told us you had found a
company with over 13,000 retail locations that still loses money, the only "list" we would
add it to would be the "Companies That Are Too Ridiculous to Possibly Exist" list.

Arguably the most important category when deciding the winner of the "All-Hype" team
award is the "ability to successfully engineer a short squeeze". Goldman, yet again,
demonstrated excellence in this category with the addition of Freshpet to its coveted
"Conviction List" a few weeks ago.


This is one of the few instances in which we understand the logic of their analysis. With
days to cover currently sitting at 42, and an upcoming lockup expiration on May 6, we
understand that the underwriters of FRPT needed to do something to keep the stock price
alive and healthy ahead of the lockup expiry, so that the secondary is successfully priced
at above IPO price (it never looks good for secondary offerings to price below the IPO).


According to reports, the lead sponsor in the Freshpet deal is currently actively fundraising
for its next private equity fund. Limited partners always like to see realizations as part of a
PE fundraising process. Given the pet food company valuation framework we laid out
above, we believe that any prudent fiduciary will be jamming down the sell button the
moment the lockup expiration hits. So expect even more positive sell-side coverage
through the May 6 lockup expiration, including reports with titles such as "Freshpet
Freezer Expansion Gives Short Sellers the Chills."


You may also be wondering why the sell side and management harped on velocity
improvements so strongly following the recent earnings call. Velocity - which the sell side
appears to be measuring by looking at year-over-year changes in revenue per fridge - had
been slowing up until the 4th quarter. While velocity appears to have improved in the most
recent quarter, we point out that management did not really provide much color on how or
why the velocity improved. One way velocity can improve is simply by cramming more pet
food into the fridges (which would accelerate sales into the channel, but has no impact on
retail sell-through). This would make near-term velocity look like it is optically improving
(because Freshpet's measured revenues are based on wholesale revenues rather than
actual consumption at retail). Perhaps, however, advertising campaigns are working, and
the business is actually seeing some positive momentum. An IPO can often help raise
short-term awareness, for example.


So we caution investors to do their homework before reading too much into headline
velocity improvement.

The sell-side analysts covering Freshpet have done anything to refute a "velocity
deceleration" short thesis. Why would that be? Simple: to change the focus of the
discussion.


When did the "short thesis" on Freshpet solely hinge on a "velocity deceleration" pitch?


Velocity is just one small component of the short thesis on Freshpet. It also happens to be
the variable that is most readily under management's control, and the variable that is most
likely to upside surprise in the first quarters following an IPO (how many other IPOs have
we seen flame out after a few quarters of strong growth out the gate). In other words,
shifting the discussion to velocity is a fantastic way to distract an audience.
